# Case Study: Cyberbullying

## Type of Cybercrime
Cyberbullying

---

## What Is Cyberbullying?

Cyberbullying is the use of digital platforms — such as social media, messaging apps, online games, or email — to repeatedly harass, threaten, humiliate, or harm another person. Unlike traditional bullying, cyberbullying can happen 24/7 and reach a victim anywhere, making it particularly damaging.

---

## How It Typically Happens (Step-by-Step)

1. **Targeting begins** – The bully identifies a victim, often a peer, classmate, or someone they have a personal conflict with.
2. **Platform selection** – The bully uses Instagram, WhatsApp groups, Twitter/X, or online gaming chats to reach the victim.
3. **Harassment starts** – This may begin with mocking comments, spreading rumours, or sharing embarrassing photos without consent.
4. **Escalation** – Other users may join in (pile-on effect), making the victim feel isolated and powerless.
5. **Anonymity** – Bullies often use fake accounts or anonymous profiles to avoid accountability.
6. **Continued targeting** – The victim cannot "escape" because the harassment follows them online even at home.

---

## Who Is Targeted?

College and school students aged 14–22 are most frequently targeted, especially those who are new to a social group, LGBTQ+ individuals, students with visible differences, or anyone who speaks up publicly online.

---

## Consequences

- Severe psychological impact: anxiety, depression, low self-esteem
- Academic performance decline
- Social withdrawal and isolation
- In extreme cases: self-harm or suicidal ideation
- Legal consequences for the bully under India's IT Act, 2000 and IPC Section 507
